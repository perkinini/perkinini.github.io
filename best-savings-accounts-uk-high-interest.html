Best Savings Accounts UK: Where to Find High Interest Rates in 2025

If your cash is still sitting in a high-street bank earning next to nothing, you're essentially letting inflation quietly pick your pocket. Whether you're building an emergency fund, saving for a house deposit, or simply want your money to work harder while you sleep, this guide breaks down the best savings accounts UK high interest options available right now, and how to choose the right one for your situation.

Why High Interest Savings Accounts Matter Right Now

The Bank of England base rate has been at levels we haven't seen since the early 2000s. While that's been painful for mortgage holders, it's genuinely good news for savers — if you know where to look.

The catch? Many of the big traditional banks (we're looking at you, Barclays and HSBC) have been notoriously slow to pass rate increases on to their savings customers. Meanwhile, challenger banks and savings apps have been offering rates that put them firmly to shame.

The difference is stark. A basic instant-access account at a legacy bank might offer 1–2% AER, while the best easy-access accounts on the market are currently offering 4.5–5%+ AER. On a £10,000 balance, that's the difference between earning £150 a year and earning £500. Over time, that compounds significantly.

Types of Savings Accounts to Consider

Before diving into specific products, it's worth understanding the main account types, because the "best" account really depends on how you plan to use your savings.

Easy Access Savings Accounts

These let you deposit and withdraw money whenever you like, making them ideal for emergency funds or shorter-term goals. The trade-off is that rates can change at any time. Right now, competition in this space is fierce, with several providers offering north of 4.5% AER.

Fixed-Rate Bonds

If you can lock your money away for 1–5 years and don't need to touch it, fixed-rate bonds typically offer the highest interest rates. The downside is that your cash is tied up — early withdrawal usually isn't possible, or comes with a significant penalty.

Cash ISAs

A Cash ISA lets you save up to £20,000 per tax year completely free of income tax. With HMRC's Personal Savings Allowance (£1,000 for basic rate taxpayers, £500 for higher rate), many savers don't currently need an ISA — but if you're a higher earner or have substantial savings, they're worth considering.

Notice Accounts

A middle ground between easy access and fixed-rate. You earn a better rate than a standard easy-access account, but you need to give notice (typically 30–95 days) before withdrawing. Good if you're disciplined but want some flexibility.

Tips for Maximising Your Savings Interest

  • Use your ISA allowance first if you're a higher-rate taxpayer or have large savings balances.
  • Automate your savings — the best savers pay themselves first. Apps like Chip make this almost invisible.
  • Ladder your savings — split money between easy access (for emergencies) and fixed-rate (for long-term savings) to maximise returns without sacrificing flexibility.
  • Check rates regularly — the savings market moves fast. What was best in January may not be best in June.
  • Don't forget the FSCS limit — keep no more than £85,000 with any single banking group to stay fully protected.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the highest interest rate available on UK savings accounts right now?

Rates change frequently, but as of 2025, the best easy-access accounts are offering around 4.5–5%+ AER, while fixed-rate bonds for 1–2 years can exceed 5% AER in some cases. Always check a comparison site for the latest figures.

Is my money safe in a savings app like Chip or Monzo?

Yes — both Chip and Monzo are FCA regulated and your eligible deposits are protected by the FSCS up to £85,000 per banking licence. This is the same level of protection you'd get with a traditional high-street bank.

Do I pay tax on savings interest in the UK?

It depends. Basic rate taxpayers get a Personal Savings Allowance of £1,000 per year; higher rate taxpayers get £500; additional rate taxpayers get nothing. Interest above these thresholds is taxable and should be declared to HMRC via a Self Assessment tax return if applicable. A Cash ISA is always tax-free, regardless of how much interest you earn.

How often do savings rates change?

Easy access account rates can change at any time and providers are only required to give you 14 days' notice of a rate reduction in most cases. Fixed-rate bonds are locked for the agreed term. It's worth reviewing your savings accounts every 3–6 months to ensure you're still getting a competitive deal.

Should I choose an easy access account or a fixed-rate bond?

This depends on your situation. If the money might be needed at short notice (e.g., it's your emergency fund or you're saving for something within 12 months), choose easy access. If you have savings you genuinely won't need for 1–5 years, a fixed-rate bond will almost always pay you more.
